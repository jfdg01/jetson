# Academic Literature Survey: Natural-Language-Commanded Drone Object Following on a Jetson Orin Nano 8 GB

## TL;DR
- The exact system you describe — a VLM running at 0.5–3 Hz on a Jetson Orin Nano 8 GB that turns "follow the white car" into a bounding box/direction for an autopilot — has **no published end-to-end equivalent**; the closest analogs are modular grounding-and-track papers, aerial referring-expression datasets (RefDrone, UAVNLT, AerialMind), and zero-shot VLM-waypoint navigators (See, Point, Fly). This is a genuine, defensible thesis gap.
- The right architecture for your hardware is **modular, not end-to-end VLA**: monolithic VLAs (OpenVLA 7B, π0 3B) do not fit or run fast enough, while a small grounding VLM (SmolVLM-256M/500M) emitting a bounding box, handed to a lightweight Kalman/IoU tracker (ByteTrack) and a velocity/IBVS controller, matches your measured throughput and the dominant pattern in the recent aerial-NL literature.
- Fine-tune a **PaliGemma-2-3B or SmolVLM-500M** base for grounded bbox output (PaliGemma has native `<locXXXX>` detect tokens and a GGUF/llama.cpp path; SmolVLM is already inside your measured budget), train on **RefDrone + UAVNLT** plus VisDrone/UAVDT auto-labeled with a large VLM, and report tracking AUC/precision, latency (Hz), resident memory, power (W), and end-to-end following error.

## Key Findings

1. **Field sparseness is the headline result.** There is active work on (a) VLAs for manipulation, (b) NL navigation for drones, and (c) NL-specified tracking — but almost nothing that fuses all three into an on-device, real-time "refer-and-follow" controller on an 8 GB embedded module. The pieces exist; the integration is the contribution.
2. **Monolithic VLAs are the wrong tool for this hardware.** RT-2 (55B), OpenVLA (7B), π0 (3B), and Octo (27–93M but JAX/diffusion) target manipulators, not UAVs, and assume workstation GPUs. The June 2026 vla.cpp paper confirms π0 and GR00T variants either barely fit or do not fit on the Orin Nano 8 GB.
3. **The modular pattern dominates aerial NL work.** See, Point, Fly (CoRL 2025), the PX4 agent paper (2025), and AirHunt (2026) all separate a VLM "grounder/reasoner" from a classical controller — exactly your proposed design, and exactly what your 0.5–3 Hz budget forces.
4. **Aerial NL grounding datasets now exist** (RefDrone, UAVNLT, AerialMind), all built on VisDrone-family imagery with semi-automated LLM-assisted annotation — both a data source and a methodological template for your auto-labeling.
5. **Lightweight trackers are mature and tiny.** ByteTrack (ECCV 2022) and Siamese CPU trackers (SiamTPN, 30+ FPS on one CPU core) run far faster than your VLM, so the autopilot/tracker can comfortably hold track between 0.3–3 Hz VLM updates.
6. **There is a GGUF-class VLA runtime now** (vla.cpp, June 2026), but its native sub-8-bit quantization is limited to BitVLA's ternary path; for your purposes a PaliGemma/SmolVLM grounding VLM in standard GGUF Q4/Q8 is the more reliable route.

## Details

### 1. Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models for robotics

**RT-2** — *RT-2: Vision-Language-Action Models Transfer Web Knowledge to Robotic Control*, Brohan/Zitkovich et al. (Google DeepMind), CoRL 2023 (arXiv 2307.15818). Built on PaLI-X / PaLM-E backbones (up to 55B). Represents actions as text tokens: 6-DoF end-effector positional + rotational displacement, gripper extension, and a discrete termination command, with each continuous dimension discretized into 256 uniform bins (8 integers per action). **Takeaway:** Established the "actions-as-tokens" paradigm you can reuse for emitting discretized bbox/direction tokens, but the model scale is irrelevant to edge use.

**OpenVLA** — *OpenVLA: An Open-Source Vision-Language-Action Model*, Kim, Pertsch, Karamcheti et al., CoRL 2024 (arXiv 2406.09246). 7B params; SigLIP + DINOv2 fused vision encoder into a Llama-2-7B backbone; trained on 970k Open X-Embodiment trajectories; discrete action tokens. It outperforms the closed RT-2-X (55B) by 16.5 absolute points across 29 tasks with 7× fewer parameters, is fine-tunable via LoRA on consumer GPUs, and quantizes well: per the paper's Table 2, int4 reaches 71.9 ± 4.7% BridgeData V2 success at 7.0 GB VRAM versus bfloat16's 71.3 ± 4.8% at 16.8 GB (int8 drops to 58.1 ± 5.1%), over 80 rollouts. **Takeaway:** The most important open VLA and the methodological reference for your fine-tune (LoRA + 4-bit serving), but 7B is too large for a 6.4 GB resident ceiling at usable speed.

**π0 (Pi-Zero)** — *π0: A Vision-Language-Action Flow Model for General Robot Control*, Black, Brown, Driess et al. (Physical Intelligence), 2024 (arXiv 2410.24164). PaliGemma-3B backbone (2.291B fine-tuned) + a 0.315B action expert producing continuous actions via **flow matching** at up to 50 Hz, action chunk size 50; trained on 7 robot embodiments / 68 tasks. π0-FAST uses a DCT transform to compress actions into discrete tokens. **Takeaway:** Demonstrates continuous-action flow matching and chunked open-loop replay — conceptually parallel to your "autopilot holds track between updates" — but it is a manipulation model and the 3B + expert is heavy for the Nano.

**Octo** — *Octo: An Open-Source Generalist Robot Policy*, Octo Model Team (Ghosh, Walke, Pertsch, Black et al.), RSS 2024 (arXiv 2405.12213). Transformer + diffusion action head, 27M and 93M checkpoints, trained on 800k OXE trajectories; supports language and goal-image conditioning and chunked diffusion actions. **Takeaway:** The parameter counts (27–93M) are the only VLAs small enough to be edge-relevant, but it is JAX/Flax with a diffusion head (no GGUF path) and manipulation-focused.

**PaliGemma-based VLAs / backbone lineage** — π0 and many others build on **PaliGemma** (SigLIP + Gemma), which natively supports detection (`detect` prefix → `<locXXXX>` tokens) and referring-expression segmentation. **Takeaway:** PaliGemma is the single most reusable backbone for you because the same model family is both a proven VLA backbone *and* a native bounding-box grounder.

**UAV-specific VLAs:** **UAV-Flow Colosseo** (Wang et al., 2025, arXiv 2505.15725) — "Flying-on-a-Word," the first real-world benchmark for language-conditioned fine-grained UAV control via imitation learning; benchmarks a "Pi-0-UAV" (flow matching, 6-DoF, chunk size 10) and finds VLA models outperform VLN baselines, with spatial grounding the critical factor. **GRaD-Nav++** (2025, arXiv 2506.14009) — a VLA-styled drone navigation policy with directional + object outputs. **Takeaway:** UAV VLAs are nascent (all 2025+), confirm grounding is the bottleneck, and none target an 8 GB module at 0.5–3 Hz.

**Smallest/most efficient VLA & the GGUF question:** Per *vla.cpp: A Unified Inference Runtime for Vision–Language–Action Models* (Nguyen, Ho, Nguyen, Duong, Le, D.M.H. Nguyen, Ngo, A.T. Le; VinRobotics / VinUniversity / TU Darmstadt / Max Planck / Stuttgart / DFKI; arXiv 2606.08094, 6 Jun 2026, v1 preprint) — the first ggml/GGUF-class runtime to natively serve flow-matching/diffusion VLA heads. It serves seven architectures (SmolVLA-450M, Evo-1-770M, BitVLA-2.4B, π0-3B, GR00T-N1.5/1.6/1.7-3B) from a single self-contained GGUF bundle (LM + action head + normalization stats + config; vision encoder embedded or as a companion `mtmd` multimodal file). On the **Jetson Orin Nano 8 GB**, only 5 of 7 load; measured per-step latencies (ms) with peak RSS: SmolVLA 141.8 ms / 2031 MiB, BitVLA 355.7 ms / 2199 MiB, Evo-1 458.8 ms / 2135 MiB, GR00T-N1.5 84.8 ms / 5975 MiB, π0 39.1 ms / 6068 MiB; GR00T-N1.6/1.7 (~6 GiB weights) do not fit. **BitVLA** runs at 100% LIBERO-Object success in ~1.3 GiB via ternary two-bit packing (on-disk 5.6 → 1.34 GiB), and a custom W2A8 IMMA tensor-core GEMM cut BitVLA per-step latency ~4.0–4.6× across devices. **Two critical caveats:** (a) vla.cpp's native low-bit quantization is limited to BitVLA's ternary path — all other models run in **BF16**, so there is *no* stated Q4/Q8 GGUF offering for π0/SmolVLA/GR00T; (b) a FP16 vision-tower position-embedding rounding bug displaced predicted actions by up to ≈1.97 in robot space until made precision-aware (position-embedding relative error 0.32 → 0.003). **No official GGUF export exists for OpenVLA, π0, or SmolVLA as complete action-producing policies** — only their VLM backbones are independently in GGUF; the one community 4-bit (Q4_K_M) GGUF VLA, **LiteVLA-Edge** (~150 ms / ~6.6 Hz on AGX Orin), supports only a single autoregressive architecture and no flow/diffusion head. **Takeaway:** If you ever want an end-to-end VLA on the Nano, vla.cpp + a SmolVLA-class model is the only realistic path today, but it is bleeding-edge, BF16-bound for non-ternary models, author-reported, and manipulation-trained — which reinforces the modular recommendation.

### 2. Natural-language grounding in aerial / drone imagery

**RefDrone** — *RefDrone: A Challenging Benchmark for Referring Expression Comprehension in Drone Scenes*, Sun, Liu, Zhu, Gu, Zou, Liu, Xia, Du, Xu (Wuhan University), 2025 (arXiv 2502.00392). Contains 17,900 referring expressions across 8,536 images, comprising 63,679 object instances, built on VisDrone imagery. It reveals three challenges: 31% small objects and 14% large objects (multi-scale); multi-target and no-target samples where an expression can refer to anywhere from 0 to 242 objects; and complex contextual expressions (color attributes appear in 69% of expressions, with 12 color terms). Built with **RDAgent**, a semi-automated multi-agent (LLM) annotation framework; the paper proposes NGDINO (Number GroundingDINO). **Takeaway:** The single best (image, NL expression, bbox) aerial dataset for your fine-tune, and its multi-agent annotation pipeline is a directly reusable auto-labeling template.

**UAVNLT** — *A Benchmark for UAV-View Natural Language-Guided Tracking*, MDPI Electronics 13(9):1706, 2024. UAV videos from four cities; vehicles annotated with bounding boxes, trajectories, and NL sentences encoding category, color, position, and direction of movement (explicitly to overcome the low appearance-uniqueness of similar UAV-view targets). **Takeaway:** The closest existing analog to your task (UAV-view, NL → track a vehicle); small but directly on-point for evaluation.

**AerialMind** — *AerialMind: Towards Referring Multi-Object Tracking in UAV Scenarios*, Chen, Liang et al., AAAI 2026 (arXiv 2511.21053). First large-scale referring multi-object tracking (RMOT) benchmark for UAVs; built with **COALA**, a multi-stage LLM-collaborative semi-automated annotation framework; proposes HawkEyeTrack (HETrack) with a Co-evolutionary Fusion Encoder and Scale-Adaptive Contextual Refinement. **Takeaway:** The most recent and most directly aligned with "refer and follow"; cite as the state of the art that still targets offline benchmarks, not on-device real-time.

**Why aerial is hard for ground-trained VLMs:** RefDrone and VisDrone literature document extreme scale variance, top-down/oblique viewpoints, very small objects (VisDrone has a nontrivial fraction at 8×8 px; "tiny" defined as <32×32 px), dense scenes (VisDrone median ~43 labels/image, max 914), motion blur, and weak context; RefDrone reports that current SOTA models "perform poorly on RefDrone compared to ground-level datasets." **Takeaway:** These are precisely the failure drivers you should evaluate against; do not assume RefCOCO-level grounding transfers to aerial.

### 3. Visual object tracking (VOT) + language on drones

**ByteTrack** — *ByteTrack: Multi-Object Tracking by Associating Every Detection Box*, Zhang et al., ECCV 2022 (arXiv 2110.06864). Associates *every* detection (including low-confidence) via Kalman + IoU; 80.3 MOTA / 77.3 IDF1 / 63.1 HOTA on MOT17 at 30 FPS on a V100. **Takeaway:** The most practical tracker to sit below your VLM — robust to occlusion, tiny footprint (motion + IoU, no heavy ReID network), and trivially fed a VLM-selected initial box.

**Aerial MOT integrations:** RAMOTS (2025) reports HOTA 48.14 at 28 FPS for UAV aerial MOT with ByteTrack; comparative UAV studies find StrongSORT + YOLOv8 best for accuracy/robustness and ByteTrack best for track continuity in dense scenes. **Takeaway:** ByteTrack is the pragmatic default; StrongSORT if you need appearance ReID and can afford it.

**Lightweight Siamese single-object trackers:** **SiamTPN** — *Siamese Transformer Pyramid Networks for Real-Time UAV Tracking*, Xing & Tsoukalas, WACV 2022 (arXiv 2110.08822) — ShuffleNetV2 backbone, >30 Hz on a single CPU core (45 FPS with ONNX/OpenVINO), 58.1% AUC on LaSOT, designed explicitly for drone deployment. **SiamHSFT** (Sensors, 2023) — 126.7 FPS average across UAV123/UAV20L/DTB70. **Takeaway:** If you prefer single-object template tracking over detection+association, these CPU-real-time trackers leave the entire GPU free for the VLM.

**NL-specified tracking front-ends:** TNL2K — *Towards More Flexible and Accurate Object Tracking with Natural Language*, Wang, Shu, Zhang, Jiang, Wang, Tian, Wu, CVPR 2021 (arXiv 2103.16746) — 2,000 sequences, 1,244,340 frames, one English sentence + per-frame bbox per video; the foundational NL-tracking benchmark, adding adversarial-sample and modality-switch challenges. Joint vision-language trackers (SeqTrackv2, SUTrack, CiteTracker) show NL improves robustness but add parameters. **Takeaway:** End-to-end NL trackers exist but are heavier than your budget; the modular "VLM picks the box once → cheap tracker maintains it" is the better fit and is the dominant practical pattern.

**UAV tracking benchmarks & numbers:** UAV123 (Mueller, Smith, Ghanem, ECCV 2016) — 123 sequences, 110K+ frames, low-altitude aerial; SiamRPN++ ~0.613 success, modern trackers ~0.61–0.68 success and ~0.80–0.89 precision. DTB70 (70 sequences, severe camera motion), UAVDT (SOT subset 50 sequences / 37,084 frames), VisDrone-SOT. **Modular vs end-to-end tradeoff:** modular (VLM grounding → classical tracker) is debuggable, swappable, and cheap but suffers error hand-off and re-ID gaps; end-to-end learned is tighter but data-hungry and rarely edge-deployable. **Minimum viable tracker:** ByteTrack-style Kalman+IoU (the BYTE association logic itself is sub-100 MB and CPU-capable) is the lightest competitive option; a Siamese ShuffleNetV2 tracker is the lightest single-target template tracker.

### 4. Edge deployment of VLMs for real-time robotics

**SmolVLM** — *SmolVLM: Redefining Small and Efficient Multimodal Models*, Marafioti, Zohar et al. (Hugging Face), 2025 (arXiv 2504.05299). 256M/500M/2.2B; SigLIP encoder + SmolLM2; aggressive pixel-shuffle compresses each 384×384 patch to 81 tokens (a single image + prompt is ~1.2k tokens vs ~16k for Qwen2-VL). Per the paper, SmolVLM-256M "uses less than 1GB GPU memory during inference and outperforms the 300-times larger Idefics-80B model" (0.8 GB single-image VRAM vs 27.7 GB for MolmoE-A1B-7B). Apache 2.0, fully open training pipeline. **Takeaway:** This is your primary edge VLM family — your own measurements (256M @ 3.29 Hz / 1777 MB; 500M @ 2.96 Hz / 2241 MB) confirm it fits comfortably under the 6.4 GB ceiling with headroom for the tracker and camera pipeline.

**Jetson VLM guidance:** The NVIDIA Technical Blog (Jan 2026) states the "Jetson Orin Nano 8GB is suitable for VLMs and LLMs up to nearly 4B parameters, such as Qwen2.5-VL-3B, VILA 1.5–3B, or Gemma-3/4B" — matching your finding that Gemma-4-E4B (6444 MB) causes swap. The llama.cpp Jetson ecosystem confirms CUDA sm_87 builds (`-DGGML_CUDA=ON -DCMAKE_CUDA_ARCHITECTURES=87`) and documents a recent MoE-decode regression on sm_87 (Issue #19219) — pin a known-good llama.cpp commit. **NVIDIA TensorRT Edge-LLM** (2026) is an alternative C++ runtime with NVFP4 quantization and EAGLE-3 speculative decoding, but currently targets Jetson Thor / JetPack 7, not the Orin Nano. **Takeaway:** Your runtime choice (llama.cpp + GGUF, sm_87, 15 W) is well-supported and the correct call; budget for llama.cpp version pinning.

**Quantization strategies:** GGUF K-quants (Q4_K_M, Q8_0) are the llama.cpp standard; QLoRA's NF4 4-bit (Dettmers, Pagnoni, Holtzman, Zettlemoyer, *QLoRA: Efficient Finetuning of Quantized LLMs*, NeurIPS 2023, arXiv 2305.14314) "reduces memory usage enough to finetune a 65B parameter model on a single 48GB GPU while preserving full 16-bit finetuning task performance" via NF4 + double quantization + paged optimizers; AWQ/GPTQ are common for serving. **Takeaway:** Train in QLoRA NF4, merge, export to GGUF Q4_K_M or Q8_0; expect near-lossless accuracy if done carefully, but validate grounding accuracy *post-quantization* (the vla.cpp position-embedding bug is a cautionary tale that vision-tower precision matters disproportionately).

### 5. Fine-tuning small VLMs for structured grounded output

**PaliGemma fine-tuning for detection/grounding** is the best-documented recipe: the model emits `<locXXXX>` tokens (coordinates normalized to a 1024×1024 grid, 4 digits each) in a JSONL format; RefCOCO is the standard referring-expression training set; LoRA/QLoRA + BitsAndBytes 4-bit is the standard memory-efficient path (HF TRL cookbook "Fine-tuning a VLM for Object Detection Grounding," Roboflow JSONL tooling). The PaliGemma-2 tech report (Steiner et al., 2024, arXiv 2412.03555) notes classical detection AP is *harder* for VLMs than REC due to a metric/objective mismatch — relevant since you care about single-target REC, not full-scene AP. **Takeaway:** PaliGemma's native location-token vocabulary means you can teach bbox-from-NL with a simple text-target fine-tune; the format and tooling are mature.

**SmolVLM/LLaVA/Idefics structured output:** SmolVLM can be LoRA-fine-tuned for structured/JSON output; the HF cookbook demonstrates the exact RefCOCO grounding pipeline. **Takeaway:** If you stay inside your measured 256M/500M budget, SmolVLM is the base; if you can tolerate ~3B in Q4, PaliGemma-2-3B gives stronger native grounding.

**QLoRA/LoRA for VLMs:** community and survey reports put QLoRA within ~0.5 points of full fine-tune on many benchmarks with up to ~75% memory savings; DoRA generalizes low-rank adaptation to VLM benchmarks. **Aerial fine-tuning sources:** RefDrone is the natural choice; VisDrone/UAVDT provide bboxes but need NL synthesis (Section 6). **GGUF export after LoRA merge:** merge adapters into base weights *before* running `convert_hf_to_gguf.py`; the fiddly step for multimodal models is exporting the vision projector as the `mmproj` companion file — test the merged FP16 model end-to-end before quantizing. **Takeaway:** QLoRA → merge → GGUF is standard and well-trodden for LLMs; multimodal GGUF export is the error-prone step, so validate the `mmproj` file explicitly.

### 7. System architecture patterns

**Modular (recommended; dominant in aerial NL work):** NL + frame → VLM grounder (bbox or 2D waypoint) → tracker (ByteTrack/Siamese) → controller → MAVLink/PX4. Exemplars: **See, Point, Fly** (Hu, Lin, Lee et al., CoRL 2025, arXiv 2509.22653) reframes action prediction as a 2D spatial-grounding task — the VLM annotates a 2D waypoint, unprojected to a 3D displacement via camera intrinsics, with an adaptive controller compensating for VLM latency (reported "≈1–3 s" inference) and closed-loop dynamic-target *following* on a DJI Tello. Across 23 simulated and 11 real-world tasks it reports 93.9% success in simulation and 92.7% real-world, versus TypeFly (0.9% / 23.6%) and PIVOT (28.7% / 5.5%). **Taking Flight with Dialogue** (Lim et al., 2025, arXiv 2506.07509) integrates PX4 + ROS2 + Ollama-served LLMs/VLMs; Gemma3/Qwen2.5/Llama-3.2 produced 100% valid flight commands, VLMs gave 97–100% valid binary object-presence responses, and the best mission success was 40% (Gemma3 pairing). **AirHunt** (2026, arXiv 2601.12742) bridges VLM semantics with continuous planning for aerial object navigation. **Takeaway:** Every recent aerial NL system is modular and uses the VLM as a slow, high-level grounder/reasoner over a fast classical controller — strong precedent for your design and your 0.5–3 Hz budget.

**End-to-end learned (not recommended here):** RT-2/OpenVLA/π0/UAV-Flow map image + NL → action tokens directly; superior coupling but data-hungry, large, and (per vla.cpp) hard to fit/run on the Nano.

**Control interface:** PX4 or ArduPilot over **MAVLink**, typically via ROS/ROS2; offboard velocity setpoints (body-frame vx, vy, vz, yaw-rate) are the natural target for a "follow" task. Note the PX4 NED vs ROS ENU frame conversion (flagged as a real limitation in the PX4 agent paper). **Controllers:** image-based visual servoing (IBVS) and PID on bbox-center error + bbox-area-for-range are the standard for follow tasks; See, Point, Fly uses an adaptive step-size controller; MPC appears in higher-end systems. Given 0.5–3 Hz vision, a **cascade** is appropriate: a fast inner-loop attitude/velocity controller (autopilot, ~50–400 Hz) under a slow outer-loop PID/IBVS on the tracked box (vision rate), with the *tracker* (not the VLM) providing the high-rate target estimate between VLM updates. **Re-ID after occlusion:** ByteTrack recovers short occlusions via low-score association; longer losses need re-detection (re-invoke the VLM grounder) or appearance ReID (StrongSORT) — the literature treats long-occlusion re-ID as substantially unsolved for aerial small objects.

### 8. Open problems and honest failure modes

- **Re-ID after long occlusion / out-of-frame** is the most-cited unsolved problem, acute for small aerial objects with weak appearance features (AerialMind and UAVNLT both motivate NL partly to disambiguate look-alike targets).
- **Ambiguous/under-specified NL** ("the white car" when several exist) — RefDrone explicitly includes multi-target and no-target cases (0–242 referents); most trackers assume exactly one target.
- **OOD aerial viewpoints**: VLMs trained on ground imagery degrade on top-down/small/blurred objects (RefDrone reports SOTA models "perform poorly" vs ground-level).
- **Latency/staleness**: at 0.5–3 Hz the VLM box is always stale; the autopilot/tracker must extrapolate, and fast targets can outrun the update rate. vla.cpp's open-loop-chunk analysis and See, Point, Fly's adaptive controller both exist precisely to manage this.
- **Quantization fidelity**: the vla.cpp FP16 vision-tower position-embedding bug (action error up to ≈1.97 in robot space, silently, until fixed) shows reduced precision can corrupt grounding — validate post-quantization.
- **Sim-to-real & evaluation gaps**: the PX4 agent paper notes missing latency/path-optimality metrics, no motion-blur/exposure artifacts in sim, and small real-world trials; most aerial NL papers evaluate offline on benchmarks, not on-device closed-loop.
- **Common unstated assumptions**: a single unambiguous target, good GPS/visual odometry, daylight/clear weather, modest target speed, and that ground-trained grounding transfers to aerial — all questionable outdoors.

### 9. Concrete recommendations (what to reuse directly)

1. **Starting checkpoint:** Two-track decision. (a) *Conservative, in-budget:* **SmolVLM-500M** (your measured 2.96 Hz / 2241 MB) — Apache 2.0, GGUF-native via llama.cpp `mmproj`, leaves ample room for tracker + camera. (b) *Stronger grounding:* **PaliGemma-2-3B** in Q4_K_M — native `<locXXXX>` detection tokens, proven REC, GGUF-exportable; expect roughly 1–2 Hz on the Nano (interpolating between your Gemma-4-E2B 0.49 Hz and SmolVLM points), so verify it clears your minimum control rate. Prototype on SmolVLM-500M, then A/B against PaliGemma-2-3B for grounding accuracy vs rate.
2. **Dataset:** Fine-tune primarily on **RefDrone** (real aerial image + expression + bbox), augment with **UAVNLT** for the vehicle-following/video case. Preprocessing: tile/crop high-res frames to preserve small objects (sliced inference à la SAHI), pad to square at the backbone's native resolution (384/448/896), normalize boxes to the model's coordinate convention; augment with scale jitter, brightness/exposure variation, and synthetic motion blur to mimic flight.
3. **Auto-labeling recipe:** Replicate RefDrone's **RDAgent** / AerialMind's **COALA** pattern on VisDrone/UAVDT: take existing bbox + class + attribute labels, use a large VLM (e.g., Qwen2.5-VL-72B or a GPT-class model) to caption each crop and generate referring expressions, encode color/position/motion from bbox + track metadata, then template-expand and LLM-paraphrase. Filter with a round-trip check (feed the generated expression back to a grounder and keep only triplets whose predicted box matches the source at IoU > 0.5).
4. **Downstream tracker:** **ByteTrack** (Kalman + IoU + low-score association). Footprint: detector-dependent, but the BYTE association logic itself is sub-100 MB and CPU-capable, leaving the GPU for the VLM. Use the VLM box to initialize/re-seed the track; ByteTrack maintains identity at video rate between VLM updates. If single-target CPU-only tracking is preferred, use a **SiamTPN**-class ShuffleNetV2 tracker (30+ FPS on one CPU core).
5. **Controller:** A **cascade** — outer-loop PID/IBVS on bbox-center pixel error (yaw + lateral) and bbox-area error (range/forward), running at the tracker's rate, feeding body-frame velocity setpoints to PX4/ArduPilot over MAVLink offboard; inner-loop attitude/velocity handled by the autopilot. Add an adaptive step-size/gain (à la See, Point, Fly) to remain stable under variable VLM latency. PID is the right complexity for a master's thesis; MPC is optional future work.
6. **Evaluation metrics:** (a) *Grounding/tracking:* success/AUC and precision on UAV123/UAVNLT/RefDrone splits, plus IDF1/MOTA if multi-object; (b) *Systems:* end-to-end latency and VLM throughput (Hz), resident memory (MB), power (W) at 15 W TDP, swap incidence; (c) *Following:* end-to-end following error (target-to-image-center pixel error or metric standoff error over time), track-loss rate, re-acquisition time after occlusion; (d) *Ablations:* base model, quantization level (FP16 vs Q8 vs Q4) vs grounding accuracy, and VLM update rate vs following error.
7. **Related-work positioning / the gap:** Prior work covers (i) large VLAs for manipulation (RT-2, OpenVLA, π0, Octo), (ii) aerial NL navigation/grounding evaluated offline (RefDrone, AerialMind, UAVNLT, See, Point, Fly), and (iii) edge VLM/VLA deployment (SmolVLM, vla.cpp, NVIDIA Jetson). **No published system closes the loop: an on-device sub-3-Hz grounding VLM driving a real-time tracker + autopilot for NL-commanded object *following* on an 8 GB embedded module.** Your thesis fills exactly this intersection — a quantified, edge-constrained, modular refer-and-follow system with honest latency/power/accuracy characterization.

## Recommendations (staged)

1. **Now — reproduce the budget and baseline.** Confirm your SmolVLM-256M/500M GGUF numbers and stand up a zero-shot baseline (SmolVLM bbox → ByteTrack → PID/MAVLink in SITL). **Threshold to proceed:** end-to-end loop ≥1 Hz with stable hold on a slow target in simulation.
2. **Next — fine-tune for grounding.** QLoRA-fine-tune SmolVLM-500M (and PaliGemma-2-3B as the accuracy ceiling) on RefDrone + UAVNLT; merge and export to GGUF Q4_K_M/Q8_0; validate grounding accuracy *after* quantization. **Threshold:** post-quant REC accuracy within ~3 points of FP16, and grounding precision high enough that the tracker re-seeds correctly ≥90% of the time.
3. **Then — auto-label to scale data.** If RefDrone + UAVNLT prove insufficient, run the RDAgent/COALA-style pipeline on VisDrone/UAVDT with round-trip filtering. **Threshold:** measurable AUC/precision gain on a held-out UAVNLT split.
4. **Finally — close the loop on hardware and characterize.** Move from SITL to the Orin Nano + airframe; report the full metric suite (Section 9.6). **Decision points that change the plan:** if PaliGemma-2-3B cannot clear your minimum control rate on-device, fall back to SmolVLM-500M; if long-occlusion re-ID dominates failures, add StrongSORT appearance ReID or a VLM re-detection trigger; if quantization degrades grounding, ship Q8 instead of Q4 or keep the vision tower at higher precision.

## Caveats
- **The exact end-to-end system is unpublished**, so several recommendations are synthesized across adjacent fields rather than drawn from a single drop-in precedent; this is the thesis's opportunity but also means fewer turnkey baselines.
- **vla.cpp (arXiv 2606.08094)** is a June 2026 v1 preprint with author-reported, not-yet-independently-replicated numbers and no peer-reviewed venue; treat its Orin Nano figures as indicative.
- **On-device PaliGemma-2-3B throughput on the Orin Nano is an interpolation**, not a number I verified; benchmark it before committing.
- **Dataset licenses vary by mirror** (VisDrone is CC BY on some mirrors, CC BY-SA on the Voxel51 Hugging Face mirror; RefDrone/UAVNLT/AerialMind are research-use per their repos) — verify before redistribution.
- Several cited UAV NL benchmarks are very recent (2025–2026) and may have limited third-party validation; cross-check their splits before reporting headline numbers.
- GGUF multimodal export (the `mmproj` vision-projector file) is the most error-prone step in the toolchain; budget time to validate the merged model end-to-end before quantizing.
